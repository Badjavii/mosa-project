# Philosophy

Every software project carries a vision and a philosophy that define its intentions and its commitment to the people who use it. MOSA is no exception. This document outlines the core software philosophies that shaped this project, and that we believe are worth sharing with both new and experienced programmers.

## Open Source Software

For a long time, software has been treated as a black box — its internal workings, architecture, and technologies hidden from the outside world, and those who did know were bound by confidentiality agreements not to share them.

This lack of transparency naturally breeds distrust. Users have no way of knowing what a program does internally, which means vulnerabilities can go unnoticed for years. The strength of open source is precisely this: it only takes one programmer anywhere in the world to find a bug, report it, and get it fixed. Transparency is not just an ethical choice — it is a security advantage.

There is also the question of ownership. When you buy a physical object, it belongs to you. You can modify it, repurpose it, or take it apart. Software rarely works this way. Even after paying for it, users have no real ownership — and any attempt to modify it can result in legal consequences. Open source challenges this model by returning software to the people who use it. It does not belong to its manufacturers; it belongs to the community that builds and sustains it.

## Free Software

Free software is both a philosophy and a critique of the way software has evolved as a commercial product. Much of today's software can be described in three patterns: services that are nominally free but saturated with advertising, artificial restrictions that degrade the user experience unless lifted by paid upgrades, and the aggressive collection and monetization of user data.

It is not unreasonable for someone to build a business around software. What is worth criticizing, however, are the methods used to extract revenue — methods that often treat users as the product rather than the beneficiary.

The fundamental purpose of software should always be the same: to improve human life. Free software holds that this purpose is incompatible with pop-up windows that expose users to malware, with features locked behind paywalls that serve no purpose other than to extract money, and with data practices that surveil rather than serve. Software built with genuine intention does none of these things. It is open, honest, and useful — not despite being free, but because of it.
